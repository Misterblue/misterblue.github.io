MisterBlue's Book: 2001

Ahhhh.... the sweet memories...

Introduction to Matrix Theory and Linear Algebra
by Irving Reiner
You got the subject from the title.  Good text book for a college class.  I needed some refreshing.  (December 2001)

1066 : The Year of the Conquest
by David Howarth
 This is the way history should be written about.  Unlike the Conquest of Constantinople which merely lists the battles and troop movements, David Howarth describes the people and the society and puts the actions, motivations and outcomes into a very human context. (October 2001)

The Alchemist: A Fable About Following Your Dream
by Paulo Coelho, Alan R. Clarke (Translator)
If you are in a time of your life where you are ready for the message of this simple, roughly constructed fable, this book can be life changing.  Any other time, the story will make you mad because of it's simple construction, pedantic style and obvious message.  The basic message is about personal fate and how a person and their dreams ("personal legend") fit into the mechanisms of fate. (October 2001)

The Rough Riders
by Theodore Roosevelt,
Roosevelt was not one to sit on the sidelines, so, when the task of driving the Spanish colonialists from the Americas arose, Mr. Roosevelt quit his government job, formed a volunteer soldier cavalry, and went off to Cuba to fight.  A rousing tale. The man is full of himself and the manliness of it all, but the Ivy college boys would discuss Balzac before they went into battle while the cowboys broke horses. They took San Juan Hill amidst a rain of bullets and came home victorious. Bully! (October 2001)

The Conquest of Constantinople
Author: Geoffroy de Villehardouin
A translation of an old text -- a description of the forth Crusade written by one of the participants.  Actually one of the first historical documents written in the vernacular.  I like to learn about the time and the people through history but this didn't do that.  It was a fairly dry recounting of this battle and that battle and this person getting land and then fighting for it.  Save this for the "original sources"  section of your thesis bibliography. (October 2001)

Mouse Tales : A Behind-The-Ears Look at Disneyland
by David Koenig
 David Koenig interviewed past and present employees of Disneyland and filled this book with anticdotes ranging from the mundane (the guest from hell) to the prurient (the equivalent of the Mile High Club for the room at the top of the Matterhorn). The problem is that the book reads like it's a bunch of interview notes placed end-to-end.  If you are a Disney freak, you'll find the stories interesting.  If not, this book gives very little insight into the company or the experience of working there. (September 2001)

My House, My Paradise
by Gustau Gili Galfetti, Gustav Gili, Gustau Galfetti Gili
 I'm going to have a house like this someday!!  (September 2001)

Into Thin Air : A Personal Account of the Mount Everest Disaster
by Jon Krakauer
 My wife gave me this book. She's a new marathon runner and she finds tales of physical exertion and trial interesting. As for me, I'm wondering at people who, for fun not less, stretch themselves to their limits and beyond. This is a story by a mountain climbing maniac fulfilling his dream of climbing to the top of Everest and, along the way, watching others die in the attempt. Well written and evocative of the beauty and trials of extreme mounting climbing. (September 2001)

Significant Others: The Ape-Human Continuum and the Quest for Human Nature
by Craig B. Stanford
  For someone who spends months at a time alone with gorillas, Stanford writes very clearly and expressively. The book is in three sections: what does primate behavior say about us, what's wrong with the established views of evolutionary psychology, and what's happening with gorillas in the wild.  While Dr. Stanford has strong views on all of these topics, he writes clearly about the other views on the subject.  I like this writing style because it puts his views in context, educates me on the pivotal differences with this topic, and gives me proper grounding to form my arguments if I don't happen to agree with him.  All in all, a good popularization of this topic. (August 2001) 

Java in a Nutshell : A Desktop Quick Reference (Java Series) (3rd Edition)
by David Flanagan
Well written.  Good intro. (July 2001)

The Sound of Waves
by Yukio Mishima, Meredith Weatherby (Translator)
Great writing.  Mishima is known for his simple but deep story telling.  Life in a Japanese fishing village.  Young love and jealousy.  No car crashes or international plots... and right triumphs in the end. (July 2001)
Cartoons of the Roaring Twenties
by R. C. Harvey
Volumes one and two.  They saw the world differently then and expressed humor differently.  It seems that he wasn't able to finish the series.  Too bad. (June 2001)

Timeline
by Michael Crichton
While an interesting story, this book was very disappointing  I haven't read any of Crichton's other books so I don't know if this is just a weak one or what.  The characters were strong in the beginning but want flat in the end (especially the professor).  Maybe that's how he writes books -- strong characters driving the plot in the beginning then action driving the plot later.  But the plot wasn't constructed subtly... the structure and skeleton of the story stuck out (we have to say this now so it will support the story later....).  (June 2001)

Mostly Harmless
by Douglas Adams
 Some of the inventions are wonderful, as always.  I love Adams' imagination. But the book ends weakly and the story is not very well held together.  The Vogons again plot to destroy the earth but they have to take over the Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy's publishing company.  The book was fun but not fulfilling. (June 2001)

The Mutiny On Board H. M. S. Bounty
by William Bligh
This was not what I expected.  I had seen the movie and expected a rousing tale of power and order.  But this is the log book of a very staid and purposeful ship's captain.  The H.M.S.Bounty was one of England's exploration ships discovering and charting these far away islands so the main purpose of the captain's log was to record navigation points, plants and natives.  Some of  the crew didn't want to go back to England and mutinied to stay on the islands, and this story is a very dry telling of Captain Bligh's trip with his few remaining crew in a small boat. (May 2001)

Dune : House Atreides (Dune Series)
by Brian Herbert, Kevin J. Anderson (Afterward), Frank Herbert
 Excellent, excellent book.  Well written, well paced and well integrated with the Dune stories.  A "must read" for Dune fans. (April 2001)
Coffin for Dimitrios
by Eric Ambler
 A wonderful, exotic 30's detective mystery.  The "detective" is an English mystery writer who, while researching for one of his books, gets entangled with a real international criminal (the Dimitrios in the title).  Well, actually he is introduced to his corpse and spends the books meeting all the interesting characters in continental Europe who inhabited Dimitrios' world. (April 2001)

The Animal Factory
by Edward Bunker
An interesting story of life inside prison.  Well told.  A look into what it's like having to live inside in walls for a prison for years and years and years.  As the title implies, prisons don't create civilized citizens to release back into the real world. (March 2001)

Manchu Palaces : A Novel
by Jeanne Larsen, Susan Clark (Narrator)
Chinese mystical tail of ghosts, gods and the imperial household. An interesting look into another world but I found the story hard to follow at time.  It also seemed to have some sex scenes added to make the publisher happy or something -- they didn't fit and didn't add anything to the story. (February 2001)

Under the Streets of Nice
by Ken Follett, Rene L. Maurice, Roddy McDowall (Reader)
A bank in Nice, France was tunneled into from the sewers and many millions of francs were stolen.  And they got away with it.  This is a reporters story of the people and the execution of the other great robbery of the century.  Well written and interesting. (February 2001)

The Salaryman's Wife
by Sujata Massey
 A nice little murder mystery set in Japan.  While well written and a nice first book for a continuing series with the same female lead character, the mystery was contrived with a solution revealing clue in the last few chapters. (February 2001)
