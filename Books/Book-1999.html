MisterBlue's Books: 1999 and before

Ahhhh.... the sweet memories...


 Changing Places : A Tale of Two Campuses
by David Lodge
(Nov 1999)

The Death of Common Sense : How Law Is Suffocating America
by Philip K. Howard

He has a point (the list of laws that are getting in the way of doing the right thing) but I expected a description of the problem followed by something that could be done or maybe an analysis of the causes of suffocating law creation and the mechanisms that perpetuate them.  Instead, this book lists one horror story after another and ends with a whinny "we've got 'a break free".  After this book, I have a better list of examples of the problem but I don't have any tools to fix it or any insight into it.  (Nov 1999)

The Animal Wife
by Elizabeth Marshall Thomas
Out of print book in mold of Clan of the Cave Bear and Reindeer Moon (the latter being Elizabeth Thomas' first book).  An interesting look into life for early man written by an anthropologist. Some of the books about mankind's beginnings are more about change and the coming of the future, this story is just about life and living and what is important to these people long ago.  (Dec 1999)

The Magician's Wife
by Brian Moore
A story of illusion, faith and intrigue told by the wife of a magician who is asked by his king to help him subdue the Algerian Arabs and further the French empire.  Wonderfully told.  (Dec 1999)

Lolita [UNABRIDGED]
by Vladimir Vladimirovich Nabokov, Jeremy Irons (Reader)

This book is great literature.  I'm amazed at Nobokov's command of the English language and this audio tape (it makes my commute easier) is read by Jeremy Irons who starred in the latest remake of this book.  He is perfect.  But the story is difficult because I bounced between "this is so wonderfully written" and "but she's only 12".  The story describes a man's deep passions in ways not usually done in most writings.  But the object of his imaginings and passions is the 12 year old girl, Lolita.  A "girl-child".  But, as happens in all situations like this, the "girl" is really more of a "child" and can not live up to the older man's expectations.  The writing is transcendent but the story is painful and tragic. (Nov 1999)

The Holy Man's Journey
by Susan Trott

I picked this up because it was small.  It's a story of a holy man who lives in a hermitage on a mountain and who is taking the person he's training to be his replacement on a journey to the man who was his teacher.  The wonderful part of this story is the character of the holy man.  I really felt the different outlook on the world and the different approach to life.  This book made me look at the world differently. (Oct 1999)

After Many a Summer Dies the Swan
by Aldous Huxley

After reading several contemporary works, this was a refreshing return to "old fashioned" writing where the reader needs some knowledge of history and literary stories with a touch of Latin and French to catch the references that the author creates around the story.  The story is about the clash of traditional culture with the nouveau rich in Los Angeles in 1938.  There is opulent and arbitrary riches alongside great poverty. (Oct 1999)

The Samurai's Garden
by Gail Tsukiyama

A even toned story of a Japanese young man recovering from an illness in the families beach cabin during world war II.  The "samurai" of the title is actually the old man who tends the families cabin and the story is about the young man's relationship with this housekeeper and the housekeeper's old love who is a leper in a village near the cabin.  The story is about long term relationships and their quiet strength set against the transient ness of the far away world at war.  A comfortable read but it could have been set in New England for all of the local detail. (Aug 1999)

Cold Mountain : A Novel
by Charles Frazier

This much hyped best seller was given to me for Christmas and I loved the first few chapters.  The book opens in a civil war hospital and the narration is in the head of the fellow recovering from wounds.  I loved the beginning tone and prose.  It truly captured that voice that goes on in one's head -- what one notices and what one thinks.  The male protagonist leaves the hospital and we shift to the female protagonist back on the farm on Cold Mountain.  Her situation is odd since she lead a rather protected childhood and is now in charge of the farm.  Then the story gets odder and odder.  The lady takes up with this quiet, sullen woman who knows all of the ways of  plants and animals and the fellow runs across murderous priests and even a witches coven.  Really odd stuff that, for me, really left me wondering if I wanted to finish the book or not.  A quick, obtuse happy ending and the oddness comes to an end.  I did not enjoy this book nearly as much as it's popularity promised. (Aug 1999)

Guns, Germs, and Steel : The Fates of Human Societies
by Jared Diamond

This is a wonderful book that I haven't yet come up with the words to describe it.  Maybe some email would help.  (June 1999)

Memoirs of a Geisha
by Arthur S. Golden

I've always been interested in Japanese history and culture and read this book to get a look into the world of the Geisha.  The story is set just before and during World War II just as the Geisha culture and system was winding down.  The book follows the life of a high class geisha (real geisha were hostesses and sex had very little to do with it).  A girl comes from a small country village, enters a house of geishas in training and rises to be one of the elite.  The tone of the book is understated and even with few ups and downs.  The story is interested and gives a view of becoming a geisha from the girl's point of view. (July 1999)

Power Living by Jake : Eleven Lessons to Change Your Life
by Jake Steinfeld
A get-yourself-moving self help book.  Jake is a personal trainer for the stars and he peppers this book with name dropping and stories about his sessions.  I liked this book because Jake's effervescent energy shines through.  After a chapter or two, you wish you could meet this man.  He's positive, energetic and going somewhere.  He'd be fun to be around.  That energy comes through in his book and it lead me to.... well...  self help.  (May 1999)

The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind
by Julian Jaynes

A "must read" for anyone thinking about the definition and operation of consciousness.  Jaynes may not have the answer, but he makes you think with his interesting thesis on when and why man became conscious.  (May 1999)

The Czar's Madman : A Novel
by Jaan Kross, Anselm Hollo (Translator)

(Feb 1999)

A Nation of Salesmen : The Tyranny of the Market and the Subversion of Culture
by Earl Shorris

Mr. Shorris is a retired ad executive who has stepped back and looked at what he has wrought.  The book is an interesting read and it makes some points that should give anyone pause to think, but others with more literary and/or philosophic power have also examined the history of commercial selling, it's impact on our society and, eventually, how we value things, people and our lives. (Nov 1998)
